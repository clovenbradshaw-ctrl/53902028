1. The Big Picture: What Is This?

You're looking at public spending data from Metro Nashville's Office of Homeless Services (OHS). Specifically, you're seeing invoices and payments made using federal COVID relief funds.

In Simple Terms

The federal government gave Nashville money to help recover from COVID-19. The city council passed a resolution (RS2024-490) directing almost $10 million of that money toward two things: running a new homeless housing facility (Strobel Center) and funding affordable housing construction (Barnes Fund). The invoice viewer lets you see how that money is being spent.

Important: This Is One Specific Allocation

This invoice viewer does not show all of Nashville's ARPA spending. Nashville received approximately $260 million in ARPA funds spread across many programs. What you're seeing here is spending from one specific allocation (business unit 53902028) that was originally designated for the Strobel Center and later expanded via RS2024-490 to include Barnes Fund Round 13 grants. To see all Metro ARPA expenditures, you would need to query other business units in fund 30216.

Why Does This Matter?

This is public money being spent on public programs. Understanding where it goes helps citizens:

  • Track accountability — Is the money going where the council said it should?
  • Understand government — How does Nashville actually pay for services?
  • Identify concerns — Are there unusual patterns or potential issues?

2. What Are ARPA Funds?

ARPA stands for the American Rescue Plan Act, a $1.9 trillion federal stimulus package signed into law in March 2021. It provided direct relief to state and local governments to respond to COVID-19.

Key Facts About ARPA

  • Nashville received approximately $260 million in ARPA funds
  • The money must be obligated by December 31, 2024
  • All funds must be spent by December 31, 2026
  • Funds can be used for COVID response, economic recovery, replacing lost revenue, and infrastructure

Because these are federal funds, they have stricter tracking requirements than regular city money. That's why they're separated into their own fund code: 30216 (the ARPA Fund).

Nashville's Original Homeless Services ARPA Allocation (October 2022)

Before RS2024-490 existed, Metro Council approved Mayor Cooper's "Housing First" plan on October 5, 2022, distributing $50 million across four core resolutions:

Resolution Amount Purpose
RS2022-1696 $25,000,000 Gap Financing for Permanent Supportive Housing (administered by MDHA)
RS2022-1697 $9,000,000 Housing First Supportive Services (wraparound services only)
RS2022-1698 $9,000,000 Temporary Interim Gap Housing (includes Rodeway Inn operations)
RS2022-1699 $7,000,000 Low Barrier Housing Collective (landlord incentives and competitive grants)

A separate $18 million went to Metro Planning Department through RS2022-1860 ($10M for collective/shared equity housing) and RS2022-1861 ($8M for aging-in-place housing). These funds were not originally designated for homeless services—they became available for reallocation when the Planning Department programs failed to materialize.

3. Resolution RS2024-490 Explained

A resolution is a formal decision made by the Metro Council. Resolution RS2024-490, adopted on June 4, 2024, is the authorization that created business unit 53902028.

What the Resolution Did

It took money from two earlier ARPA allocations that weren't being fully used:

Source Original Purpose Amount Moved
RS2022-1860 Collective/Shared Equity Housing $8,439,446
RS2022-1861 Shared Housing for Seniors $1,200,000

And redirected those funds to new purposes:

Strobel Center

$4,400,000

Operating expenses and supportive services for Nashville's first public permanent supportive housing facility.

Barnes Fund R13

$5,239,446

Grants to nonprofit developers building affordable housing through Round 13 of the Barnes Housing Trust Fund.

Why Were the Original Funds Available?

None of the $10.7 million in RS2022-1860/1861 funds had been spent prior to reallocation. Metro Planning Department stated these funds would be "better utilized" in homeless services, though questions remain about why programs were originally appropriated for initiatives that never materialized.

4. Eligible Users: Who Can Receive These Funds?

Federal ARPA funds have specific eligibility requirements. Understanding who qualifies helps identify whether money is reaching intended populations.

Strobel Center Eligibility (45 + 45 Units)

First 45 Units — Chronically Homeless Individuals

Must meet HUD's chronic homelessness definition:

  • Homeless continuously for at least 1 year, OR
  • Homeless on at least 4 separate occasions in the last 3 years (episodes totaling at least 12 months)
  • AND has a disabling condition (mental illness, substance use disorder, physical disability, or developmental disability)

Second 45 Units — Priority Populations

  • Unhoused veterans
  • Young adults (18-24) experiencing homelessness
  • LGBTQ+ individuals experiencing homelessness

Documentation Required

  • VI-SPDAT Assessment Score (Vulnerability Index)
  • Documentation of chronic homelessness status
  • Social Security Card, ID, and/or Birth Certificate
  • Disability eligibility verification
  • Cannot be on sex offender registry

Critical Access Requirement

All Strobel Center placements must go through OHS's Coordinated Entry process—a standardized assessment system that prioritizes housing based on vulnerability scores. Direct placements outside Coordinated Entry violate program design.

Barnes Fund Round 13 Eligibility

Barnes Fund grants go to nonprofit developers, not individuals. However, the housing they build must serve:

  • Households at 0-30% Area Median Income (AMI) experiencing homelessness
  • Properties must use Coordinated Entry Process managed by OHS for tenant selection
  • PSH projects must follow Housing First approach (no preconditions for entry)
  • 30-year affordability requirement on all units

Historical Eligibility Compliance Concern

Metro's own 2022 Performance Study found that only 26% of individuals moving into Permanent Supportive Housing had been homeless for one year or longer—falling significantly short of the chronic homelessness duration requirement. High levels of missing data in HMIS (Homeless Management Information System) made eligibility verification difficult to audit even before current oversight concerns emerged.

6. Business Units Demystified

A business unit is like a separate bank account within Metro's accounting system. Each major allocation of money gets its own business unit so spending can be tracked separately.

How to Read Business Unit Numbers

For Department 053 (OHS), the 8-digit codes follow a pattern:

Pattern Fund Type Examples
531XXXXX General Fund (city's regular budget) 53101000
533XXXXX Federal Grants (HUD, etc.) 53312000
537XXXXX Special Purpose Funds 53701500
539XXXXX ARPA Funds 53902025, 53902026, 53902027, 53902028

The Business Unit You're Looking At

53902028 is specifically for the RS2024-490 allocation. The "539" prefix tells you it's ARPA money, and "02028" is the sequential number for this specific allocation.

9. The Strobel Center

The Strobel Center is Nashville's first public permanent supportive housing facility, located at 1210 Murfreesboro Pike. It opened on July 18, 2024.

Key Facts

  • 90 units for chronically homeless individuals
  • Operated by Depaul USA under contract with Metro
  • Provides housing plus wrap-around supportive services
  • Named after Jim Strobel, longtime homeless services advocate

What the Money Pays For

The $4.4 million allocation covers "supportive services and operating expenses," including:

  • Property management — maintenance, security, utilities
  • Case management — housing navigators, tenant support
  • Mental health services — counseling, psychiatric care
  • Substance abuse treatment — recovery support
  • 24/7 staffing — front desk, overnight coverage

10. Strobel Center Oversight Timeline

The Strobel Center experienced significant procurement and oversight failures that are important context for understanding invoice data.

Chronology of Events

Date Event
May 2022 Construction began; $35M total cost (40% over original estimate)
May 28, 2024 Metro Council approves initial 4-month contract with Depaul USA
June 4, 2024 RS2024-490 passes, allocating $4.4M for operations
July 18, 2024 Strobel House grand opening
September 2024 Initial contract expires—OHS continues payments without renewal
Sept 2024 – Aug 2025 10+ months operating without valid contract
~May 2025 OHS Director learns of procurement non-compliance
August 19, 2025 Contract lapse publicly disclosed; Council approves $3.5M emergency contract (RS2025-1436)
September 5, 2025 First payment under new contract

How the Voucher Bypass Worked

During the contract lapse, OHS paid Depaul USA through direct vouchers rather than purchase orders. This circumvented Metro Finance oversight:

"What happened in this case is that payments were being made to DePaul through a direct voucher system. The direct voucher payment does not connect to a contract, and finance does not approve those direct vouchers. Those are all approved at the department level." — Mary Jo Wiggins, Metro Finance

Standard purchase orders are connected to contracts and would have prevented payments on an expired contract. The voucher system allowed department-level approval without Finance review.

Budget Overruns

  • Original startup budget: $500,000 approved by Metro Council
  • Actual spending: Over $1 million billed by time of discovery
  • Overrun: $500,000-$700,000 more than initially budgeted

This led to supplemental appropriation RS2025-1436 for an additional $3.5 million.

What This Means for Invoice Data

Invoices from September 2024 through August 2025 were processed without contract oversight. These payments may appear normal in the data but were made outside standard procurement controls.

11. The Barnes Fund

The Barnes Housing Trust Fund is Nashville's competitive grant program for nonprofit housing developers. It provides capital funding to build affordable housing.

How It Works

Key Characteristics

  • Grants, not loans — developers don't repay the money
  • Reimbursement basis — nonprofits document expenses, then get paid back
  • 30-year affordability — properties must remain affordable for 30 years
  • No PSH services — funds are for construction, not ongoing supportive services

Round 13 (R13)

The $5.2 million from RS2024-490 went into Barnes Fund Round 13, awarded in September 2024. In total, R13 distributed $37.9 million to 16 nonprofit organizations, funding 1,164 affordable housing units.

What You'll See in the Data

Barnes Fund payments will appear as grants to nonprofit developers like Habitat for Humanity, Woodbine Community Organization, or Affordable Housing Resources. The object code will typically be 505303 (Contribute/Grant Person) or similar.

The "Non-Traditional Rapid Rehousing" Question

OHS operated a program placing families in hotels and apartments with Metro paying rent directly—described internally as "Non-Traditional Rapid Rehousing." This program:

  • Bypassed Coordinated Entry protocols
  • Used unspecified "bridge funding"
  • Operated without identified Council authorization
  • Expanded to $2.4 million in FY2026

Unauthorized Expenditures Identified

Invoice analysis of BU 53902028 reveals $361,871 in apparent Non-Traditional Rapid Rehousing payments—expenditures that were NOT authorized under RS2024-490. This represents an estimated 77-105 households and approximately 258 household-months of housing assistance at ~$1,400/unit/month.

13. Federal Compliance Requirements

Because these are federal ARPA funds, Nashville must follow U.S. Treasury rules in addition to Metro's own policies.

Treasury SLFRF Final Rule Requirements

The State and Local Fiscal Recovery Funds (SLFRF) program allows homeless services spending, including:

  • Permanent Supportive Housing operations
  • Non-congregate shelter (private rooms vs. dormitories)
  • Affordable housing development
  • Hotel/motel master leases for shelter expansion

However, recipients must comply with:

2 CFR Part 200 (Uniform Guidance)

  • Competitive procurement for contracts exceeding threshold amounts
  • Documentation of procurement rationale, contractor selection, and pricing
  • Subrecipient monitoring with written agreements and risk assessment
  • Cost allowability principles

Single Audit Requirements

Nashville's $259.8 million ARPA allocation triggers mandatory Single Audit requirements. This means federal auditors can examine any ARPA expenditure for compliance.

Key Deadlines

  • Obligation deadline: December 31, 2024 (funds must be committed)
  • Expenditure deadline: December 31, 2026 (all spending must be complete)

Federal Clawback Risk

Any funds not properly spent by the expenditure deadline must be returned to the federal government.

14. Subsequent Modifications to RS2024-490

The original RS2024-490 allocation has been modified by subsequent Council actions.

RS2025-1436 — Strobel Center Supplemental ($3.5 Million)

Adopted: August 19, 2025

Purpose: Emergency contract funding after the 10-month contract lapse was discovered. Covers:

  • Property management and resident support services
  • Retroactive authorization for voucher period spending
  • Continuation through new fiscal year

Notable Provision: Includes media coordination clause requiring Depaul USA to coordinate any media events or news releases through OHS.

RS2024-240 — Barnes Fund Transfer ($7.55 Million)

Adopted: April 2024

Transferred unobligated ARPA funds from RS2022-1696 (Affordable Housing Gap Financing) to Barnes Housing Trust Fund for Round 14. New eligibility requirements:

  • Rental housing for 0-30% AMI households experiencing homelessness
  • Must utilize Coordinated Entry Process
  • PSH projects require Housing First approach

Pending: Metro Audit

In September 2024, the Metro Audit Committee approved Councilmember Ginny Welsch's request for a full OHS audit, citing potential irregularities totaling $3.2 million:

  • $569,000 in improper invoicing to Depaul USA
  • $565,000 improper use of landlord engagement funds
  • $130,000 in single department credit card charges
  • $2 million in temporary federal funds used for permanent positions

Impact on Invoice Data

The audit results may impact interpretation of invoice data in this viewer.
