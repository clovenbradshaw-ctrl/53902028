1. The Big Picture: What Is This?

You're looking at public spending data from Metro Nashville's Office of Homeless Services (OHS). Specifically, you're seeing invoices and payments made using federal COVID relief funds.

In Simple Terms

The federal government gave Nashville money to help recover from COVID-19. The city council passed a resolution (RS2024-490) directing almost $10 million of that money toward two things: running a new homeless housing facility (Strobel Center) and funding affordable housing construction (Barnes Fund). The invoice viewer lets you see how that money is being spent.

Important: This Is One Specific Allocation

This invoice viewer does not show all of Nashville's ARPA spending. Nashville received approximately $260 million in ARPA funds spread across many programs. What you're seeing here is spending from one specific allocation (business unit 53902028) that was originally designated for the Strobel Center and later expanded via RS2024-490 to include Barnes Fund Round 13 grants. To see all Metro ARPA expenditures, you would need to query other business units in fund 30216.

Why Does This Matter?

This is public money being spent on public programs. Understanding where it goes helps citizens:

  • Track accountability — Is the money going where the council said it should?
  • Understand government — How does Nashville actually pay for services?
  • Identify concerns — Are there unusual patterns or potential issues?

2. What Are ARPA Funds?

ARPA stands for the American Rescue Plan Act, a $1.9 trillion federal stimulus package signed into law in March 2021. It provided direct relief to state and local governments to respond to COVID-19.

Key Facts About ARPA

  • Nashville received approximately $260 million in ARPA funds
  • The money must be obligated by December 31, 2024
  • All funds must be spent by December 31, 2026
  • Funds can be used for COVID response, economic recovery, replacing lost revenue, and infrastructure

Because these are federal funds, they have stricter tracking requirements than regular city money. That's why they're separated into their own fund code: 30216 (the ARPA Fund).

3. Resolution RS2024-490 Explained

A resolution is a formal decision made by the Metro Council. Resolution RS2024-490, adopted on June 4, 2024, is the authorization that created business unit 53902028.

What the Resolution Did

It took money from two earlier ARPA allocations that weren't being fully used:

Source Original Purpose Amount Moved
RS2022-1860 Collective/Shared Equity Housing $8,439,446
RS2022-1861 Shared Housing for Seniors $1,200,000

And redirected those funds to new purposes:

Strobel Center

$4,400,000

Operating expenses and supportive services for Nashville's first public permanent supportive housing facility.

Barnes Fund R13

$5,239,446

Grants to nonprofit developers building affordable housing through Round 13 of the Barnes Housing Trust Fund.

5. Business Units Demystified

A business unit is like a separate bank account within Metro's accounting system. Each major allocation of money gets its own business unit so spending can be tracked separately.

How to Read Business Unit Numbers

For Department 053 (OHS), the 8-digit codes follow a pattern:

Pattern Fund Type Examples
531XXXXX General Fund (city's regular budget) 53101000
533XXXXX Federal Grants (HUD, etc.) 53312000
537XXXXX Special Purpose Funds 53701500
539XXXXX ARPA Funds 53902025, 53902026, 53902027, 53902028

The Business Unit You're Looking At

53902028 is specifically for the RS2024-490 allocation. The "539" prefix tells you it's ARPA money, and "02028" is the sequential number for this specific allocation.

Key Object Codes for RS2024-490

502363 - Care of Persons

This is the primary object code for Strobel Center operations. It covers contracted services for housing, case management, and supportive services provided by Depaul USA.

When you see an invoice, the object code tells you what kind of expense it was, while the vendor tells you who was paid.

7. The Strobel Center

The Strobel Center is Nashville's first public permanent supportive housing facility, located at 1210 Murfreesboro Pike. It opened on July 18, 2024.

Key Facts

  • 90 units for chronically homeless individuals
  • Operated by Depaul USA under contract with Metro
  • Provides housing plus wrap-around supportive services
  • Named after Jim Strobel, longtime homeless services advocate

What the Money Pays For

The $4.4 million allocation covers "supportive services and operating expenses," including:

  • Property management — maintenance, security, utilities
  • Case management — housing navigators, tenant support
  • Mental health services — counseling, psychiatric care
  • Substance abuse treatment — recovery support
  • 24/7 staffing — front desk, overnight coverage

Note on Oversight

The Strobel Center operated from September 2024 through August 2025 without a formal contract, using voucher payments. The budget was also exceeded by $500,000-$700,000, leading to a supplemental appropriation (RS2025-1436) for an additional $3.5 million.

8. The Barnes Fund

The Barnes Housing Trust Fund is Nashville's competitive grant program for nonprofit housing developers. It provides capital funding to build affordable housing.

How It Works

Key Characteristics

  • Grants, not loans — developers don't repay the money
  • Reimbursement basis — nonprofits document expenses, then get paid back
  • 30-year affordability — properties must remain affordable for 30 years
  • No PSH services — funds are for construction, not ongoing supportive services

Round 13 (R13)

The $5.2 million from RS2024-490 went into Barnes Fund Round 13, awarded in September 2024. In total, R13 distributed $37.9 million to 16 nonprofit organizations, funding 1,164 affordable housing units.

What You'll See in the Data

Barnes Fund payments will appear as grants to nonprofit developers like Habitat for Humanity, Woodbine Community Organization, or Affordable Housing Resources. The object code will typically be something like "Contribute/Grant Person" (505303) or similar.
